Slide 3 (Motivation)
After disasters we need fast, large-scale information about where major changes happened. Ground surveys and drones do not scale and can be dangerous right after an event.

Sentinel-2 is useful because it is free, global, frequently revisiting, and multispectral (13 bands). Those extra bands help detect material and environmental changes beyond RGB.

The challenge is that labels are limited and not all spectral change is "damage". Vegetation and illumination changes appear strongly in multispectral data, so we need methods that are robust and interpretable.


Slide 4 (Difference Subspace)
Difference Subspace is a geometric way to represent how two subspaces differ.

In our case, we take many pixel spectra from the pre-image, run PCA, and get a low-dimensional subspace Phi. We do the same for the post-image and get another subspace Psi.

DS extracts directions that are specific to the difference between Phi and Psi. Then we score each pixel by how much its pre/post spectral difference aligns with DS directions. The output is a continuous change map.


Slide 5 (Problem & project phases)
The overall goal is label-efficient change/damage mapping from multispectral satellite data.

Phase 1: compute DS and classical baselines to produce unsupervised change maps; evaluate on OSCD and visualize on MultiSenGE.
Phase 2: supervised segmentation on OSCD using raw pre/post bands, optionally adding DS/PCA-diff maps as prior channels.
Phase 3: apply the same pipeline to a true damage dataset like xBD/xBD-S12.


Slide 6 (Phase 1 section title)
Now I move to Phase 1: unsupervised change detection. The output of this phase is not just a final binary mask, but continuous score maps that can be thresholded or used as priors later.


Slide 7 (Phase 1 datasets)
We use two datasets.
OSCD: Sentinel-2 pre/post pairs with binary change masks. We use train/val/test splits for evaluation.
MultiSenGE: unlabeled Sentinel-2 data used for qualitative visualization of DS behavior over time.

Both datasets share the same preprocessing: build a valid mask for missing pixels and apply per-band z-score normalization.

Slide 9 (Phase 1 change detection pipeline)
Input is normalized pre and post Sentinel-2 stacks plus a valid mask. We apply several change detectors: DS projection, pixel difference/CVA, PCA-diff, and other classical baselines.

Each method produces a continuous score map. AUROC is computed on the continuous scores, and then we can threshold to get a binary mask for IoU/F1.
For Phase 2 we mainly save the continuous score maps as prior channels.

Slide 11 (Phase 1 results)
This slide summarizes Phase 1 quantitatively using AUROC on the OSCD test split.

Key point: PCA-diff is the strongest baseline in AUROC (0.813 in this run), and DS projection is competitive (0.755). Pixel diff/CVA is essentially tied in AUROC (0.756).
In the full classical suite, IR-MAD (0.704) and Celik (0.649) are lower in AUROC on OSCD in this run.
DS cross-residual is weaker in AUROC here, so in Phase 2 we mainly use the DS projection map as the DS prior.

If we rerun Phase 1 or change settings these numbers can move, but the qualitative takeaway remains: DS and PCA-diff produce interpretable continuous change priors.


Slide 12 (Phase 2 section title)
Now I move to Phase 2: supervised change segmentation on OSCD. Here we use OSCD ground-truth masks to train a segmentation network end-to-end.


Slide 13 (Phase 2 pipeline with priors)
The base input is what we call "raw": the Sentinel-2 bands themselves. In our setup we stack pre and post bands, so it is 13 + 13 = 26 channels (after normalization).
If we add a prior like DS projection, that prior is just one extra 1-channel score map, so raw+DS becomes 27 channels.
Optionally, we can add other Phase 1 score maps as extra channels: PCA-diff, pixel-diff, Celik, IR-MAD.

We train on 256x256 patches and predict a per-pixel probability map P(change). For evaluation we stitch patch predictions back to full tiles and compute IoU/F1 for a thresholded mask and AUROC/PR-AUC for probabilities.

Important: DS/PCA-diff are used as extra features, not as labels.


Slide 14 (Segmentation models)
We test three models.
U-Net is the baseline encoder-decoder.
ResNet-U-Net uses a ResNet-34 encoder with a U-Net style decoder to improve representation power.
PriorsFusionUNet uses separate raw and priors branches and fuses them, instead of naive concatenation.


Slide 15 (Experiments / configs)
These are the experiment settings.

Core U-Net ablation:
- E0: raw only.
- E1: raw + DS projection prior.
- E2: raw + PCA-diff prior.
- E3: raw + DS + PCA-diff priors.

Extended priors (still U-Net, to test whether "any prior helps"):
- E1b: raw + DS cross-residual prior.
- E4: raw + pixel-diff prior.
- E5: raw + Celik prior.
- E6: raw + IR-MAD prior.

We also compare alternative architectures:
ResNet-U-Net (stronger backbone) and PriorsFusionUNet (separate priors branch instead of naive concatenation).


Slide 16 (Phase 2 results)
This table reports mean IoU, mean F1, and mean AUROC on the OSCD test split.

In the current 150-epoch GPU run (single seed), adding the DS projection prior improves segmentation quality:
- E0 raw U-Net: mean IoU 0.223, mean F1 0.343, mean AUROC 0.869, PR-AUC 0.431
- E1 raw+DS U-Net: mean IoU 0.273, mean F1 0.401, mean AUROC 0.874, PR-AUC 0.436 (best IoU/F1)
- E5 raw+Celik U-Net: mean IoU 0.260, mean F1 0.382, mean AUROC 0.872, PR-AUC 0.448 (best PR-AUC)
- E2 raw+PCA-diff U-Net: mean IoU 0.202, mean F1 0.299, mean AUROC 0.755 (hurts strongly here)

Other priors did not help IoU/F1 in this run (pixel-diff, DS cross-residual, IR-MAD).

For ranking metrics, the fusion model is strongest in this run:
- Fusion raw+DS+PCA-diff: mean AUROC 0.888, PR-AUC 0.441 (IoU/F1 ~0.243/0.360)

Important note to say out loud:
these means are averaged over the 10 OSCD test cities (each city weighted equally), the mask threshold is 0.5, and this is a single-seed run. For a final paper-grade result, we would re-run multiple seeds and tune the threshold on the validation split.

Slide 18 (DS/PCA vs segmentation - Chongqing)
This slide compares Phase 1 priors and Phase 2 segmentation on the same test tile.

DS and PCA-diff highlight broad spectral change, including vegetation and radiometric shifts that are not labeled as "change" in OSCD.

The segmentation output is much sparser because it is trained to match the OSCD labels.
This mismatch explains why priors can sometimes help and sometimes hurt: they may highlight real spectral changes that are not labeled.
In the global results, DS as a prior helps overall in our current run, but the per-city behavior still varies a lot.


Slide 19 (Discussion and conclusion)
Takeaway 1: DS and PCA-diff are strong and interpretable unsupervised change detectors for multispectral Sentinel-2.
Takeaway 2: On OSCD, supervised segmentation is required to match the OSCD labels, but priors can help. In this run, adding DS projection as a prior improves mean IoU/F1 for the baseline U-Net, and Celik prior is also strong.
Takeaway 3: Fusion improves ranking metrics (AUROC/PR-AUC) even when it is not the best for F1 at a fixed threshold.
Takeaway 4: Priors are also useful for explainability, and may be even more valuable when labels are incomplete or noisy.


Slide 20 (Future work / Phase 3)
Near term: train longer, run multiple seeds, and use ImageNet-pretrained encoders to strengthen Phase 2 baselines.

Method improvements: use priors more carefully (fusion, loss weighting, or pretraining on pseudo-labels) instead of only concatenation.

Phase 3: move to a true damage dataset like xBD/xBD-S12 and predict damage categories rather than binary change.

Slide 22 (Backup slide - Phase 1 method on MultiSenGE)
If asked how DS is computed in practice:
we take pre and post pixel spectra, compute PCA bases for each image, build a DS basis from the difference between the two subspaces, and score each pixel by projecting its spectral difference onto DS.

This produces a continuous DS score map like the example shown here.
